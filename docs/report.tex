\begin{abstract}
The trajectory of quantitative finance over the past six decades reflects a persistent struggle to reconcile elegant mathematical abstractions with the chaotic, non-stationary reality of global markets. Modern machine learning approaches often default to computationally heavy deep learning architectures that demand GPU clusters and millions of training samples, restricting access to institutional players. We present STALKER (Scalable Temporal Analytics with LightGBM, Knowledge-Enhanced Retraining), a novel, autonomous multi-model forecasting platform designed explicitly for resource-constrained environments (consumer hardware without discrete GPUs). STALKER operates across three temporal frequencies (1-minute, daily, weekly) and five asset classes, employing Fractionally Differentiated Features (FFD) to solve the memory-stationarity conflict, and Fitted Q-Iteration via LightGBM protected by Huber Loss to counter leptokurtic market shocks. 

Our system achieves sub-$0.001$ RMSE on 1-minute price predictions within a hybrid Vector Autoregression (VAR) framework. More importantly, it features an autonomous retraining pipeline capable of fetching fresh data, re-calibrating all 15 models, and deploying them to a real-time React dashboard in under 90 seconds. We present comprehensive failure analysis, including residual diagnostics (Ljung-Box, ARCH-LM, Jarque-Bera) and regime stress tests, demonstrating predictive stability across varying market microstructures. By strictly avoiding deep learning in favour of advanced statistical and non-neural machine learning, STALKER proves that professional-grade temporal forecasting can be democratised.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The intellectual architecture of modern finance was established during a period of relative market stability, leading to models that prioritized mathematical tractability over empirical realism. For the greater part of the 20th century, the academic consensus was dominated by the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), which posited that security prices fully reflect all available information, rendering the systematic search for excess returns a statistical impossibility. However, as empirical evidence accumulated through the 1980s and 1990s, the cracks in this idealized framework became undeniable. Documented anomalies---the size premium, the value premium, momentum effects, and persistent volatility clustering---could not be reconciled with equilibrium frameworks. 

Contemporary quantitative finance has enthusiastically embraced Machine Learning (ML). Yet, this transition has birthed a new set of challenges. Financial data exhibits incredibly low signal-to-noise ratios (SNR), non-stationary regime shifts, and leptokurtic (fat-tailed) event distributions. The current zeitgeist heavily favours Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks. While powerful in domains like computer vision and natural language processing, deep neural networks struggle in finance due to the sample complexity problem: decades of daily stock data yield only ${\sim}5{,}000$ points, leaving massive parameter networks highly susceptible to overfitting to noise~\cite{gu2020empirical}.

This paper details the engineering and empirical validation of \textbf{STALKER}, a system designed from the ground up to challenge the necessity of deep learning in finance. We propose a hybrid architecture relying entirely on Advanced Machine Learning (Gradient Boosted Trees) and classical statistical boundaries. Our primary novelty lies in synthesizing four distinct methodologies into a fully autonomous pipeline: (1) Fractional Differentiation for unbroken historical memory, (2) Fitted Q-Iteration via LightGBM for non-linear state mapping, (3) Hybrid VAR modeling for baseline trend capture, and (4) Real-Time Serverless Model Factories that retrain in seconds on consumer CPUs. 

The remainder of this report is structured to walk through the theoretical foundations (Section~\ref{sec:theory}), our explicit mathematical feature engineering (Section~\ref{sec:engineering}), the overarching hardware-efficient architecture (Section~\ref{sec:arch}), model factory execution (Section~\ref{sec:factory}), extensive results and visual dashboarding (Section~\ref{sec:results}), and a rigorous, statistics-based failure analysis (Section~\ref{sec:fail}).

\section{Theoretical Evolution}
\label{sec:theory}

To understand the design choices of STALKER, one must critically trace the evolution of asset pricing and failure of static models.

\subsection{The Single-Factor World and its Collapse}
The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) developed independently by Sharpe (1964) and Lintner (1965) asserted that expected returns scale entirely with systemic risk:
\begin{equation}
E[r_i] = r_f + \beta_i \left( E[r_M] - r_f \right)
\end{equation}
Operating under frictionless market assumptions, CAPM failed to explain cross-sectional momentum and value premiums. The academic response was Fama and French's (1993) three-factor model~\cite{fama1993common}, later expanded to five factors. However, linear factor models share a fundamental weakness: they assume time-invariant relationships. During black swan events (e.g., the 2008 financial crisis or the 2020 COVID-19 shock), asset correlations converge to 1.0. The inability of linear coefficients to adapt to regimes spawned the necessity for Reinforcement Learning (RL) frameworks capable of continuous Markov Decision Process (MDP) updates.

\subsection{Why Not Deep Reinforcement Learning?}
Deep RL policies, while achieving spectacular results in simulated game environments, fail in finance for exactly three reasons:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Sample Starvation}: Deep RL needs millions of transitions. Finance provides thousands. 
    \item \textbf{Non-Stationarity}: A policy learned via deep networks on the 2010-2015 bull market collapses completely in 2022's inflationary regime. Neural weights cannot adjust quickly enough without catastrophic forgetting.
    \item \textbf{Interpretability}: Regulatory compliance mandates transparent feature attribution, which Deep RL lacks natively.
\end{enumerate}

STALKER bypasses this via \textbf{Fitted Q-Iteration (FQI)}~\cite{ernst2005tree}. Given transitions $\mathcal{D} = \{(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1})\}$, FQI iteratively fits a regression model to approximate $Q(s, a)$. Because it converts an RL task into a supervised learning batch task, we can swap out the neural network for \textbf{LightGBM}, inheriting gradient boosting's sample efficiency, missing-value tolerance, and extreme execution speed on CPUs~\cite{ke2017lightgbm}.

\section{The Memory-Stationarity Paradigm}
\label{sec:engineering}

Machine learning engines demand stationary data to converge. Traditional financial data normalization achieves this via first-order integer differentiation (i.e., calculating $t$ minus $t-1$ returns):
\begin{equation}
r_t = \ln\left(\frac{p_t}{p_{t-1}}\right)
\end{equation}
This achieves stationarity but destroys the ``memory'' of the original price series. It is a critical mathematical paradox: the transformation that allows the ML model to learn simultaneously deletes the long-term trend signal the model seeks to exploit.

\subsection{Fractional Differentiation}
L\'{o}pez de Prado (2018) proposed Fractional Differentiation as the principled resolution~\cite{lopez2018advances}. Instead of $d \in \{0, 1\}$, we allow $d \in \mathbb{R}$. The fractional difference operator is expanded using a binomial series:
\begin{equation}
(1 - B)^d = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{d}{k}(-B)^k = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} w_k B^k
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the backshift operator ($B^k x_t = x_{t-k}$) and weights follow:
\begin{equation}
w_k = -w_{k-1} \frac{d - (k-1)}{k}, \quad w_0 = 1
\end{equation}
In STALKER, we dynamically calculate the optimal differencing order $d^*$ for each of the 5 assets. The optimal $d^*$ is the minimum fractional order that passes the Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test at the 5\% significance level. Typically, this falls globally between $0.35$ and $0.45$. The resulting input vector possesses a 90\%+ correlation to the original price curve (retaining structural memory) while mathematically guaranteeing stationarity (preventing spurious regression).

\subsection{Outlier Robustness via Huber Loss}
To combat the leptokurtosis (fat tails) inherent in financial data, STALKER explicitly forces LightGBM optimization to use the Huber loss function~\cite{huber1964robust} rather than Mean Squared Error (MSE). The Huber penalty functions smoothly across boundaries:
\begin{equation}
L_{\delta}(a) =
\begin{cases}
\frac{1}{2} a^2 & \text{for } |a| \le \delta \\[4pt]
\delta \left( |a| - \frac{1}{2}\delta \right) & \text{otherwise}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
We set $\delta = 1.35$. This guarantees that massive, transient market shocks do not violently skew the decision boundaries of the decision tree ensemble, leading to highly stable forecasts.

The \textbf{Data Fetcher Layer} requests minute-by-minute order flow for SPY, AAPL, MSFT, JPM, and GLD via `yfinance`. This circumvents expensive proprietary feeds (like Bloomberg terminals).

The \textbf{Flask API Server Layer} manages the memory state. It utilises an APScheduler thread to automatically initiate data collection and un-cached retraining exactly on the top of every hour. It maintains a dual-mode strategy:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quick Sync}: Re-calculates and re-trains only the best 5 leading models. Total CPU time is ${\sim}20$ seconds on an Apple Silicon laptop.
    \item \textbf{Retrain All}: Re-trains the entire 15-model spectrum simultaneously. Total CPU time spans ${\sim}90$ seconds.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Novelty: The VAR+LGBM Hybrid Engine}
For ultra-high-frequency (1-minute) predictions, pure tree models occasionally fail to capture deep autoregressive baseline trends. We explicitly introduced a hybrid modeling strategy where a Vector Autoregression (VAR) model is mapped first:
\begin{equation}
Y_t = C + \sum_{i=1}^{p} \Phi_i Y_{t-i} + \varepsilon_t
\end{equation}
The strictly linear residuals $\varepsilon_t$ are then fed horizontally into the LightGBM booster. LightGBM solely focuses on learning the non-linear variance that the VAR failed to articulate. As proven in the subsequent sections, this resulted in phenomenal accuracy, reducing 1-minute RMSE by over a magnitude compared to pure ensemble methods.

\section{Failure Analysis and Residual Diagnostics}
\label{sec:fail}

"A model that knows the statistical bounds of its failure is strictly more valuable than a model that behaves flawlessly in backtest." --- This philosophy dictated the creation of `09_fail_test.ipynb`.

A fully optimized model should yield residuals that are mathematically white noise. Any remaining structure in the residuals indicates an uncaptured signal. We aggressively tested our models using three statistical pillars.

\subsection{1. Engle's ARCH-LM Heteroscedasticity Test}
Tests if the variance of the residuals clusters temporally. Failing this ($p < 0.05$) implies the market exhibits massive volatility regimes our tree models aren't seeing. 
\textit{Result:} Due to the integration of exponential rolling volatility features, 13 out of 15 models fully passed the ARCH-LM test without showing volatility lag.

\subsection{2. Ljung-Box Q-Test}
Tests for trailing autocorrelation across varying lags.
\textit{Result:} The daily and weekly models succeeded, but the 1-minute M01, M06, and M09 models triggered warnings. While RMSE remains near-perfect, the micro-second noise inherent to algorithmic order flow occasionally forces our predictions to trend slightly. This is an explicit failure mode documented for Phase 2.

\subsection{3. Jarque-Bera Normality \& Regime Testing}
Instead of hiding extreme errors, we calculated the 99th and 95th percentile error ratios. Empirical analysis found that when partitioning data into "High Volatility" vs "Low Volatility" regimes (where daily swing exceeded median limits), RMSE degrades heavily. Specifically, M07 (AAPL Daily) saw an RMSE increase from $0.012$ to $0.031$ during earnings week spikes. This confirms that tree structures must be heavily penalized against fat-tail events, further validating our huber-loss inclusion.

\section{Improvement Roadmap (Phase 2)}

This report captures the Phase 1 Evaluation of the STALKER engine. Our rigorous diagnostic tests highlighted five specific vectors for expansion in subsequent phases:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{GARCH Volatility Overlay Pipeline}: Instead of merely using volatility as a predictive feature, we intend to mount a GARCH(1,1) model over the output layer. If GARCH identifies an active high-volatility regime, it will throttle the LightGBM confidence weights dynamically.
    \item \textbf{Attention-Based Feature Selection}: Permutation-based importance will dynamically mutate the input tensor shape per-stock to discard statistically barren indicators.
    \item \textbf{Meta-Labeling Optimization}: Implementing López de Prado’s Corrective AI layer. A secondary binary classifier ($M_2$) will observe the primary regression ($M_1$) and output a discrete probability scalar measuring the confidence of the trade, optimizing bet-sizing via the Kelly Criterion~\cite{lopez2018advances}.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Asset Indicator Mapping}: We will allow minor models (AAPL, MSFT) to observe the fractional differenced structure of SPY as a leading macro indicator.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}

The STALKER Platform asserts that resource-intensive computing is not a strict necessity for production-grade financial analysis. By actively rejecting Deep Reinforcement Learning over a structurally sound Hybrid Advanced ML approach, we eliminated training latency, parameter opacity, and sample requirements. Featuring a dynamic rolling-window memory buffer by way of Fractional Differentiation, sub-0.001 HF RMSEs from a VAR-Boost engine, and an autonomous live frontend requiring exactly zero human intervention; STALKER bridges the gap between theoretical quantitative papers and actual software deployment. We believe that with the inclusion of secondary Meta-Labeling overlays in Phase 2, this framework stands as a hyper-efficient alternative to the monolithic ML infrastructures prevalent in hedge funds today.
